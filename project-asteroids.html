Summary

In this study, we leveraged NASA's Near-Earth Object Web Service (NeoWs) API to collect numerous asteroid data. This information, structured in JSON format, was efficiently stored in a MongoDB database to facilitate a comprehensive analysis. Our analysis was guided by the use of the pymongo library, which allowed us to perform a series of operations aimed at extracting valuable information from the asteroid dataset.

Our investigation focused on several critical asteroid attributes, including estimated minimum and maximum diameters, minimum impact distance, relative velocity, absolute magnitude (H), and potential hazard assessment.The main results of our study shed light on NASA's definition of potential hazard, revealing that it is less conservative than the widely accepted threshold of 0.05 astronomical units (au) or less, along with an absolute magnitude (H) of 22.0.Furthermore, our analysis revealed that the absolute magnitude (H) of an asteroid is lower than that of an asteroid. Furthermore, our analysis revealed that the absolute error associated with the estimated diameter follows an inverse power-law relationship when examined together with the absolute magnitude (H). Furthermore, we found that parameters such as distance to perihelion and semi-major axis prove sufficiently distinctive to group various orbital classes together.

These results provide valuable insights into the world of near-Earth asteroids and their characteristics, offering a deeper understanding of their potential impact hazards and the underlying relationships among key attributes.

ELT Process

The selected approach for this procedure is ETL, which stands for "Extract, Load, Transform." It encompasses a series of stages, commencing with the extraction of data from the API, followed by loading this data into the database, and culminating in the application of essential transformations. Subsequently, the transformed data is retrieved from the database for further analysis. The diagram below provides a concise overview of this process.

# Fetcher for the API
def fetcher(data):
    asteroids = {
        'id': int(data['id']),
        'name': data['name'],
        'nasa_jpl_url':data['nasa_jpl_url'],
        'absolute_magnitude_h': data['absolute_magnitude_h'],
        'estimated_diameter': data['estimated_diameter'],
        'is_potentially_hazardous_asteroid':
            data['is_potentially_hazardous_asteroid'],
        'close_approach_data':data['close_approach_data'],  
        'orbital_data':data['orbital_data'],
        'is_sentry_object':data['is_sentry_object']
    }
    
    return asteroids

Identifying Clustering Features

This section investigates the features necessary for asteroid clustering based on their orbit classes. The analysis indicates that the consideration of Semi-Major Axis and Perihelion Distance alone provides sufficient parameters for this clustering process.

In[17]:
pipeline = [
    {
        "$project": {
            "absolute_magnitude_h": 1,
            "orbital_data.perihelion_distance": 1,
            "orbital_data.semi_major_axis": 1,
            "orbital_data.orbit_class.orbit_class_type": 1,
        }
    }
]

cursor = col.aggregate(pipeline)

data = []

for entry in cursor:
    orbital_data = entry.get("orbital_data")
    absolute_magnitude_h = entry.get("absolute_magnitude_h")
    
    if orbital_data:
        perihelion_distance = orbital_data.get("perihelion_distance")
        semi_major_axis = orbital_data.get("semi_major_axis")
        orbit_class = orbital_data.get("orbit_class")
        
        if perihelion_distance and semi_major_axis and orbit_class:
            perihelion_distance = float(perihelion_distance)
            semi_major_axis = float(semi_major_axis)
            absolute_magnitude_h = float(absolute_magnitude_h)
            orbit_class_type = orbit_class.get("orbit_class_type")
            data.append({
                "perihelion_distance": perihelion_distance,
                "semi_major_axis": semi_major_axis,
                "orbit_class_type": orbit_class_type,
                "absolute_magnitude_h": absolute_magnitude_h
            })

df = pd.DataFrame(data)
min_h = df["absolute_magnitude_h"].min()
max_h = df["absolute_magnitude_h"].max()
df["normalized_absolute_magnitude_h"] = ((df["absolute_magnitude_h"] - min_h) 
                                         / (max_h - min_h))

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
for orbit_class_type, group in df.groupby("orbit_class_type"):
    marker_size = 20 + 100 * group["normalized_absolute_magnitude_h"]
    plt.scatter(
        group["semi_major_axis"],
        group["perihelion_distance"],
        label=orbit_class_type,
        s=marker_size,
    )

plt.xlabel('Semi-Major Axis (a)')

plt.title('Semi-Major Axis vs Perihelion Distance by Orbit Class Type')
plt.legend(title='Orbit Class Type')
plt.show()

Analysis of Absolute Error in Diameter Estimation

In this section, we explore the intriguing relationship between the absolute error in estimated asteroid diameter and the asteroid's Absolute Magnitude (H). This relationship reveals a distinctive pattern, akin to an inverse power-law, which signifies that as the absolute magnitude (H) varies, the absolute error in diameter estimation follows a predictable trend.

Moreover, our analysis has scrutinized the residuals obtained from the curve fitting process. The remarkably high R-squared value of 0.99 underscores the robustness and precision of the model. This strong correlation, particularly prominent within the range of absolute magnitudes falling between 12 and 22, demonstrates the model's accuracy in capturing the variations in diameter estimation error. These findings shed light on the fascinating interplay between asteroid characteristics and the precision of diameter estimations.

In[15]:
pipeline = [
    {
        "$addFields": {
            "diameter_error": {
                "$subtract": [
                    "$estimated_diameter.kilometers.estimated_diameter_max",
                    "$estimated_diameter.kilometers.estimated_diameter_min"
                ]
            }
        }
    }
]

result = col.aggregate(pipeline)

# Extract data for plotting
absolute_magnitudes = []
diameter_errors = []

for doc in result:
    absolute_magnitudes.append(doc["absolute_magnitude_h"])
    diameter_errors.append(doc["diameter_error"])

# Define the inverse power-law function
def inverse_power_law(x, a, b):
    return a * np.power(x, -b)

# Sort data for fitting
data_sorted = sorted(zip(absolute_magnitudes, diameter_errors))
x_data_sorted, y_data_sorted = zip(*data_sorted)

# Perform the curve fit
params, covariance = curve_fit(inverse_power_law, x_data_sorted, y_data_sorted)
a_opt, b_opt = params

# Calculate the fitted y values
y_fit_power_law = inverse_power_law(x_data_sorted, a_opt, b_opt)

# Create the combined plot
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))

# Scatter plot for data points
plt.scatter(absolute_magnitudes, diameter_errors, c='blue', alpha=0.5, label='Data')

# Fitted curve (Inverse Power-Law)
plt.plot(x_data_sorted, y_fit_power_law, 'r-',
         label=f'Fitted Curve (Inverse Power-Law)\na={a_opt:.4f}, b={b_opt:.4f}')

plt.xlabel('Absolute Magnitude (H)')
plt.ylabel('Diameter Error (km)')
plt.title('Absolute Magnitude vs Diameter Error')
plt.legend()
plt.show()

Conclusions

In this comprehensive study, we harnessed the NASA Near-Earth Object Web Service (NeoWs) API to collect valuable data on asteroids. Leveraging the capabilities of a NoSQL database, MongoDB, we successfully stored this data for subsequent analysis. The ETL (Extract, Load, Transform) process facilitated the seamless transition of data from the API to the database, making it readily available for in-depth exploration.

Our investigation delved into several key aspects of asteroids, including estimated minimum and maximum diameters, minimum miss distances to Earth, relative velocities, absolute magnitude (H), and the assessment of potential asteroid danger. Notably, our comparison of NASA's potential hazard standards revealed that they are less conservative than the commonly used limits, with the threshold for potential danger set at a Minimum Orbit Intersection Distance (MOID) of 0.05 astronomical units (au) or less and an Absolute Magnitude (H) of 22.0.

One of the highlights of our analysis was the identification of an intriguing inverse power-law relationship between the absolute error in estimated asteroid diameter and the asteroid's absolute magnitude (H). The associated residuals demonstrated the model's precision, especially within the absolute magnitude range of 12 to 22, resulting in a high R-squared value of 0.99. These findings provide valuable insights into the accuracy of asteroid diameter estimations.

Additionally, our exploration uncovered that the Semi-Major Axis and Perihelion Distance are sufficient features to cluster asteroids based on their orbit class, simplifying the categorization of these celestial objects.

In summary, this study offers a deeper understanding of the characteristics and classification of asteroids, as well as a robust analysis of the accuracy of asteroid diameter estimations. The rich dataset and the insights gained from this analysis lay the groundwork for future research and further our understanding of these fascinating celestial bodies.
